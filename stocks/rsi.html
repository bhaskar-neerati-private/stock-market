RSI (Relative Strength Index)

Technical Analysis • Topic 14

RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and change of price movements. It’s primarily used to identify "Overbought" or "Oversold" conditions in a stock.

Definition

  • Ranged between 0 and 100.
  • High value = Strong recent buying.
  • Low value = Strong recent selling.

Simple Explanation

  • The "Energy" Meter: It tells you how "tired" the stock is. If a stock has run up too fast too soon, its battery is drained (Overbought) and it needs to rest.
  • Emotional Gauge: High RSI = Greed. Low RSI = Fear.

Real-Life Analogy

The Rubber Band

Imagine a stock's price is a rubber band.

  • When the price stays near its average, the band is relaxed.
  • When the price shoots up (Greed), the band is stretched too tight (RSI > 70). It has a high chance of snapping back toward the middle.
  • When the price falls too fast (Fear), the band is stretched in the other direction (RSI < 30).

Key RSI Zones

Overbought (> 70)

Danger zone. Sellers are likely to take control soon. Avoid fresh buying.

Neutral (40–60)

Balanced zone. Best for long-term entries if the trend is positive.

Oversold (< 30)

Opportunity zone. Selling "panic" is likely finishing. Look for a bounce.

Thumb Rules

  • Never buy a stock when RSI > 70. You are buying other people's greed.
  • The best momentum buys happen when RSI is rising from the 40-50 range.
  • Combine with Trend: If the price is below 200-day MA, even a "low" RSI might stay low for a long time.

Ultra-Short Summary

  • RSI = Momentum Meter.
  • 70 = Too Hot (Wait). 30 = Too Cold (Watch for Bounce).
  • Helps prevent "buying at the top".
