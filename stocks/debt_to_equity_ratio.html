Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E)

Fundamental Analysis ‚Ä¢ Topic 8

The D/E Ratio measures how much "borrowed money" (Debt) a company uses compared to its "own money" (Equity). It is the ultimate test of a company's financial risk.

Definition

  • Shows the balance between what the company owes and what it owns.
  • Formula:
    • D/E Ratio = Total Debt √∑ Total Shareholders' Equity

Simple Explanation

  • Financial Leverage: Debt is like a double-edged sword. It helps you grow faster in good times but can kill you in bad times.
  • The Burden: If D/E is 2.0, for every ‚Çπ1 of their own money, the company has borrowed ‚Çπ2 from banks.

Real-Life Analogy

The College Student

Imagine two students, Rahul and Priya, both starting a business.

  • Rahul: Starts with ‚Çπ10,000 savings and borrows ‚Çπ2,000 from his dad. (D/E = 0.2) ‚Üí Conservative/Safe.
  • Priya: Starts with ‚Çπ10,000 savings and takes a ‚Çπ50,000 bank loan. (D/E = 5.0) ‚Üí Highly Aggressive/Risky.

If the business hits a bad week, Rahul can sleep at night. Priya might lose her business because she can't pay the interest.

üí° Pro Insight: Interest Coverage Ratio

D/E tells you how much debt there is. But the Interest Coverage Ratio tells you if they can actually pay it.

  • Formula: EBIT (Earnings Before Interest & Tax) √∑ Interest Expense
  • Safe Zone: > 3.0 (Means profit is 3x the interest due).
  • Danger Zone: < 1.5 (High risk of default).

Healthy D/E Zones

D/E < 0.5

Very safe. The "ideal" zone for long-term investors. Common in IT and FMCG.

D/E 1.0 ‚Äì 2.0

Moderate risk. Normal for Infrastructure or Steel companies which need heavy machines.

D/E > 2.0

High Risk. One bad quarter could lead to bankruptcy. Avoid unless it's a regulated Bank.

Special Note on Banks: Banks naturally have very high D/E (even 5.0 - 10.0) because their whole business is borrowing money from savers and lending it to borrowers. This is normal for them!

Ultra-Short Summary

  • Lower D/E = Better survival chances.
  • High Debt artificially inflates ROE.
  • Always pair with Interest Coverage Ratio.
