Face Value (FV)

Fundamental Analysis • Topic 10

Face Value (also known as Par Value) is the original nominal value assigned to a share as per the company's books. It has NO relation to the market price but is crucial for dividend calculations and stock splits.

Definition

  • The "Fixed" price of a share at its birth.
  • In India, common face values are ₹1, ₹2, ₹5, and ₹10.
  • Not for Valuation: A stock with ₹10 FV isn't "better" than a stock with ₹1 FV.

Simple Explanation

  • The Accounting Sticker: Think of it as the price printed on a stamp or a coin. The market value might change, but the printed value remains until a stock split occurs.
  • Dividend Reference: When a company says "100% Dividend," it means they are paying 100% of the Face Value, not the market price.

Real-Life Analogy

The Movie Ticket Analogy

Imagine a movie ticket with a printed price of ₹100.

  • Face Value: ₹100 (The printed price).
  • Market Price: ₹500 (The "black market" or demand price).

Even if you pay ₹500 to buy the ticket, the number printed on it remains ₹100. If the organizer decides to refund 10%, they will refund ₹10 (10% of the printed price), not ₹50.

Why It Exists

  • Stock Splits: When a stock price becomes too high (e.g., ₹10,000), the company splits the Face Value (from ₹10 to ₹1) so that there are 10 shares at ₹1,000 each.
  • Equity Capital: (Face Value × Total Shares) = Share Capital on the Balance Sheet.

    • Ultra-Short Summary

    • Face Value = Printed/Accounting value.
    • Used for Dividends and Splits.
    • Ignore it for judging if a stock is cheap or expensive.
